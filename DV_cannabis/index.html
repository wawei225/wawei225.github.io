Cannabis, Crime, and Economics

A data-driven exploration of the relationships between the prevalence of cannabis and violent crime rate and economics in Canada.

How does the prevalence of cannabis affect the violent crime rate and economics in Canada?

Cannabis has become one of the most common drugs in our society while the influence of cannabis remains debatable.

However, we should replace our fear and misunderstanding with data-supported evidence.



With organised data from Statistics Canada, we can explore areas where people are concerned with cannabis, the violent crime rate and economics. Understanding these relationships provides insights into prevention of organised crimes and reducing potential threats to societies.

Colorado has similar social and economic background as to that of Canada. Additionally, Colorado legalised cannabis in 2014 which makes it a suitable reference.

What is the trend of violent crime rate in Colorado and Canada?

Source: https://www.colorado.gov/pacific/dcj-ors/ors-crimestats
https://www150.statcan.gc.ca/n1/pub/85-002-x/2019001/article/00013-eng.htm

What have you noticed from the trend?

In Colorado, the percentage change compare to a 10-year average gradually decreased before the cannabis amendment passed and remained steady until cannabis was legalised. Interestingly, the percentage change becomes positive since 2015.


Canada obtains a similar trend where the percentage change decreases from 2008 to 2014 and starts to increase since 2014. However, the percentage change remains negative since 2013. It is important to be aware that the violent crime rate is lower than a 10-year average.

Overall, are there any specific violent sub-crimes cause the trend in Canada?

Let's look at the violent sub-crimes and compare different crime rates in Canada!

All four types of violent crime rates are decreasing !
More explicitly, the total robbery significantly decreased since 2008 but slows down after 2014; conversely, the total drug violation does not change a lot since 2008 but has been decreasing since 2011. However, are these changes due to the prevalence of cannabis?

Did you notice that the prevalence of cannabis is slightly increasing!

Cannabis use prevalence increase among adults aged 25 to 64; in contrast, the use prevalence decreased among 15 to 17-year-olds. The increasing use of cannabis in aged 25 and older, requires additional exploration to better understand preferences; however, the trend uncover that there are more aged cannabis users today than in the past.

One possible reason of the observed trend are due to more accurate reporting by a population more sensitive to the disclosure of formerly illegal drug use (legalisation of cannabis).


For each group, the prevalence of cannabis obtains similar trends for different frequencies of consuming; therefore, we will accumulate the consuming rate of different frequencies. Then, we will compare the prevalence of cannabis to violent crime rate of each age group (without separated into different frequencies).

We utilise percentage change again to compare series with different measurements.

What is the correlation between crime and prevalence of cannabis?

Interestingly, for age above 25, percentage changes of the total violent crime rate and the prevalence of cannabis has a negative relationship !

There are no significant changes of prevalence of cannabis for aged 18 to 24. The percentage changes of prevalence of cannabis is negative for aged 15 to 17. The result also supports our observation from previous chart!



For age above 25, a possible reason of observing the negative relationship is that, as Anderson and Rees (2014) point out, “there is evidence of a link between alcohol abuse and violent crime, including domestic violence [... ]. If the legalization of recreational marijuana leads to reduced alcohol consumption, we expect violent crime to fall.”(p. 228).

What have we learned so far?

For age above 25, prevalence of cannabis and violent crime rate obtain opposite trend! On the opposite, for age 15 to 17, prevalence of cannabis and violent crime rate obtain a similar trend.
There are no significant changes for age 18 to 24.


Now, we have a brief picture of the relationship between the prevalence of cannabis and crime rate.
However, how does the prevalence of cannabis affect my life? my wealth? or the economics in Canada?

Cannabis Act Passed

Did you notice that cannabis supply and prevalence of cannabis move from relatively low to relatively high? (from bottom left to top right)



Additionally, the cannabis supply has significantly increased. A possible reason is that the market anticipates an increase in demand due to the legalisation of cannabis; therefore, suppliers are likely to increase their inventory which increase the supply. The shifts of cannabis supply and the prevalence of cannabis coherence with economics theories.



The cannabis supply response to the prevalence of cannabis significantly. Will the consumer and producer price respond in the same way?

Both consumer and producer prices decreased in the past 3 to 5 years !

Based on economy theories, increase in the supply results in a decrease in the price which is indirectly linked to the anticipation of legalisation on cannabis.







How much do these changes associate with Gross Domestic Product (GDP)?


Many economics activities related to cannabis such as households' consumption, or intermediate purchases. Therefore, the gross domestic product (GDP) provides a general index for evaluating these economics activities.

Now, we have uncovered some potential correlations between supply, price, and prevalence of cannabis.

  • Anticipation of cannabis legalisation leads to the increase in the prevalence of cannabis
  • Increase in the prevalence of cannabis leads to the decrease in violent crime rate
  • Increase in the prevalence of cannabis leads to the increase in cannabis supply
  • Increase in cannabis supply leads to the decrease in cannabis price

There is a significant increase on GDP in 2018 which is highly associated with cannabis related activities such as consumption, production, and use of prevalence. The increase in GDP reflects that Canada produced more value of goods and services in 2018 compared to previous year.
